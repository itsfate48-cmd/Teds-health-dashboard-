Lab Analyst · Vibrant America Panel — 09 Apr 2025
I've cross-referenced your full panel. You have 3 high-priority flags and 4 markers to watch. Here's what's driving them and exactly what to do about each one.
🔴 High Priority — Act on These
🔥
hs-CRP 3.9 mg/L — High-Risk Inflammation (ref: ≤0.9)
You're in the high-risk cardiovascular zone (≥3.1). The good news: down from 8.3 in October — you're moving in the right direction, but 3.9 is still well above optimal. Elevated CRP is likely connected to your poor sleep (5.79 hrs last night), your elevated GGT, and chronic low-grade stress.

What to do: Target 7.5–8 hrs sleep (this alone can drop CRP significantly). Add omega-3 fish oil 2–4g EPA+DHA daily. Reduce alcohol (your GGT is also elevated). Anti-inflammatory diet: turmeric, leafy greens, olive oil, berries. Retest in 90 days — target below 1.0 mg/L.
⚡
Estradiol <10 pg/mL — Critically Low (ref: 25.8–60.7)
This has been critically low since October 2024 and is unchanged at <10. In men, estradiol is essential for bone density, cardiovascular health, libido, mood regulation, and joint protection. A value this low is associated with fatigue, brain fog, low motivation, and accelerated bone loss. This is likely your most impactful deficit.

What to do: Almost certainly connected to your critically low DHEA-S below — DHEA is the precursor. Discuss DHEA supplementation (25–50mg/day) with Dr. Austin as a first step. Ensure adequate dietary fat and zinc. Rule out any aromatase-inhibiting supplements you may be taking. Don't ignore this one.
📉
DHEA-S 15.0 µg/dL — Critically Low (ref: 44.3–331.0)
Your DHEA-S is at roughly 3% of the upper reference range — extremely low even at 53. It was 13.2 in October so it's barely moved. DHEA-S is your body's master adrenal hormone and the upstream precursor to both testosterone and estradiol. This single deficiency is likely cascading into your low estradiol, your fatigue, low readiness score (58), and poor sleep recovery.

What to do: Discuss DHEA replacement (25–50mg/day) with your provider — this is the most direct intervention. Address root causes: your sleep deficit is chronically suppressing adrenal output, reduce chronic stress, reduce alcohol. Consider ashwagandha and rhodiola for adrenal support. Getting your sleep to 7.5+ hrs is non-negotiable for recovery here.
🟡 Watch Closely
🫁
GGT 71 U/L — Elevated Liver Enzyme (ref: ≤60), down from 85
GGT is the most sensitive early marker for alcohol intake, fatty liver, and metabolic stress. Your ALT (29) and AST (21) are both healthy, so this isn't advanced liver damage — but GGT is the canary. It's improving, which is good, but still above range.

What to do: Eliminate or significantly reduce alcohol for 60 days and retest — GGT is highly responsive. Add NAC (600mg/day), milk thistle (silymarin), and eat more cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower). Avoid unnecessary supplements processed by the liver.
❤️
Homocysteine 11 µmol/L — Moderate Cardiovascular Risk (ref: ≤9)
Elevated homocysteine is an independent cardiovascular risk factor, and with your hs-CRP also elevated, your cardiac risk profile needs attention. It's been stable at 11 since October. Most commonly caused by low B12, B6, or folate — notably your B12 wasn't tested and your MCV is elevated (a classic B12 deficiency sign).

What to do: Start methylated B vitamins — methylfolate (400–800mcg), methylcobalamin B12 (1000mcg), and P5P B6 (25–50mg). Get B12 and folate levels tested at your next draw. Reduce red meat and ultra-processed foods. Target homocysteine below 7 for optimal cardiovascular protection.
🔬
MCV 95.0 fL — Mildly Elevated Red Cell Size (ref: ≤92.2)
Elevated MCV means your red blood cells are slightly larger than normal (macrocytosis). The most common causes: B12 or folate deficiency, alcohol use, or thyroid underfunction. All three are plausible given your elevated homocysteine, GGT, and Free T3/T4 sitting at the low end of range. Was also elevated in October (96.9) so this is persistent.

What to do: Test B12 and folate at your next draw — this is the most likely cause. If low, start methylated B12. Reducing alcohol will also help. Monitor Free T3 and T4 as both are at the low end of range.
🩺
Free PSA% 19.2% — Below Threshold (ref: ≥30%)
Your total PSA (0.73 ng/mL) is well within normal and stable. However, a Free PSA% below 30% statistically raises the likelihood of prostate cancer when PSA rises in future. At this stage it's not urgent but warrants annual monitoring and a PSA velocity trend over time.

What to do: Keep tracking PSA annually. Discuss PSA velocity monitoring with your provider. Lifestyle: lycopene (cooked tomatoes), zinc, selenium, regular exercise, and maintaining healthy testosterone all support prostate health.
